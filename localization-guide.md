# A0 Korean Language Course — Localization Guide

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-03-10  
**Purpose:** Cultural adaptation reference for translating the A0 Korean language course into 5 target languages

---

## 1. Overview: Philosophy of Full Cultural Adaptation

This guide governs the localization of 18 Korean-language stories (across 5 tracks: PI, TR, KD, KP, CR) into Japanese, English, Spanish, French, and Chinese.

**Core Principle:** We do *not* translate literally. We *adapt culturally.*

A learner in Tokyo should encounter a scene that feels set in Tokyo — not a Korean scene with Japanese words swapped in. A learner in Mexico City should feel at home in the dialogue, not like a tourist in Seoul reading a translated guidebook.

Concretely:
- Replace Korean apps, places, foods, and cultural references with their functional equivalents in the target culture.
- Preserve the *pedagogical intent* — the learning goal, speech level, and emotional tone must remain intact.
- Use language that native speakers actually say, not textbook-formal equivalents.
- Maintain A0 vocabulary ceiling — no complex grammar or rare words introduced.

When in doubt: **ask yourself what a local would say in this situation**, not what a Korean person would say translated.

## 2. Language Variant Specifications

Use only the specified variant for each language. Do not mix or substitute.

| Language | Variant to Use | Variant to Avoid |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Japanese** | 標準語 (Standard Japanese / Tokyo-standard) | Regional dialects (Kansai-ben, Hakata-ben, etc.) |
| **English** | American English (US) | British, Australian, or other varieties |
| **Spanish** | **Latin American Spanish** | ❌ Castilian / Spain Spanish (vosotros, leísmo, etc.) |
| **French** | **Metropolitan French** (Standard French, France) | ❌ Québécois French or other regional varieties |
| **Chinese** | **Simplified Chinese** (Mandarin, mainland PRC norms) | ❌ Traditional Chinese (Taiwan/HK conventions) |

### Practical Notes

- **Spanish:** Use *tú* / *usted* (not *vosotros*). Vocabulary should reflect Latin American usage — e.g., *computadora* not *ordenador*, *celular* not *móvil*.
- **French:** Parisian register. Avoid Québécois expressions like *pogner*, *char*, *magasiner*. Use standard vocabulary: *voiture*, *magasin*, *téléphone*.
- **Chinese:** Simplified characters throughout. Use mainland Chinese cultural references (Beijing, Shanghai, etc.). Pinyin tone marks are optional and only for pedagogical annotations, not in dialogue.

## 5. Food Mapping

### Food Scene Notes

- **Tabletop BBQ scene (삼겹살):** In Japanese, 焼肉 (yakiniku) perfectly matches the tabletop-grilling, social meal format. Use this consistently. In English, frame as "going out for BBQ" — keep the shared-meal social energy.
- **Delivery scene (치킨):** In Chinese, 炸鸡 via 美团/饿了么 (food delivery apps) is the equivalent. In English, "order fried chicken" or any delivery food works. In French, reference *Deliveroo* or *Uber Eats* as the delivery platform.
- **Café scene:** Korean café culture (specialty drinks, long stays, study sessions) maps closely to Japanese カフェ and American coffee shop culture. In France, adapt to *brasserie* or *café* with *un allongé* or *un café* — note that French café culture skews shorter visits and standing at the bar in some contexts; adapt accordingly.

## 6. Speech Level Mapping

**This section is critical.** The A0 Korean course uses different speech levels across tracks as a pedagogical feature. Translators must preserve these registers — they are not stylistic preferences, they are learning goals.

### Track Speech Level Reference

| Track | Korean Register | Register Description | Japanese | English | Spanish (LATAM) | French | Chinese |
|-------|----------------|---------------------|----------|---------|----------------|--------|---------|
| **PI** (Personal Interest) | 반말 (banmal) | Casual, between close friends/peers | タメ口 (tameguchi) | Casual register — contractions, informal vocab, no stiff phrasing | *tú* form — informal verb conjugation, casual vocabulary | *tu* form — informal register, casual expressions | 你 + casual everyday vocabulary, sentence-final particles like 吧/呢 |
| **TR** (Traveling) | 존댓말 (jondaemal) | Polite, service interactions, strangers | です・ます体 (desu/masu-tai) | Polite register — full sentences, "please", "could you", "excuse me" | *usted* form — polite verb conjugation, service-appropriate | *vous* form — formal service register, polite phrasing | 您 + polite vocabulary, please/thank-you framing |
| **KD** (K-drama) | 반말 (banmal) | Casual/emotional, dramatic peer dialogue | タメ口 (tameguchi) | Casual register — emotional, expressive, informal | *tú* form — expressive and emotional, colloquial | *tu* form — emotionally expressive, colloquial French | 你 + emotional/expressive casual vocabulary |
| **KP** (K-pop) | 반말+존댓말 혼재 | Mixed: casual with peers, polite with artists/staff | タメ口+丁寧語 (mixed) | Mixed register — casual with friends, polite with artists/venues | Mixed *tú/usted* — casual within fan groups, formal to artists | Mixed *tu/vous* — casual fan-to-fan, polite artist contexts | Mixed 你/您 — casual peer talk, polite in artist/venue contexts |
| **CR** (Career) | 격식체 (gyeogsikche) | Formal, workplace, professional | 敬語 (keigo) — 謙譲語/尊敬語 | Formal register — professional vocabulary, no contractions | Formal *usted* — professional workplace vocabulary | Formal *vous* — professional register, business French | 您 + formal business vocabulary, no filler particles |

### Speech Level Rules

1. **Do not flatten registers.** If the Korean uses 반말, the translation must be casual in the target language — even if a literal translation of the words sounds polite.
2. **Japanese:** タメ口 means dropping です/ます, using plain form verbs (食べる not 食べます), and casual sentence-enders (ね, よ, な). 敬語 means full 謙譲語 in CR contexts (いただきます, おります, ございます).
3. **Spanish:** Consistency matters — if a sim opens with *tú*, maintain *tú* throughout. Do not slip into *usted* within the same conversation unless a register shift is deliberate.
4. **French:** *tu/vous* must be consistent. In KP track (mixed), specify which party uses *tu* and which uses *vous* in translator notes.
5. **Chinese:** The 你/您 distinction must be maintained. CR track (formal) should use 您 consistently and avoid filler particles (吧/呢) that sound overly casual.
6. **English:** English lacks formal verb conjugations, so register is conveyed through vocabulary, sentence structure, and phrasing. Formal = no contractions + professional vocabulary. Casual = contractions + colloquial phrases.

## 8. Localization Rules (Summary)

Translators must follow these rules in every story:

1. **Never translate Korean cultural references literally.** Always adapt to the functional equivalent in the target culture. If there is no equivalent, choose the closest analog and add a brief translator note.

2. **Maintain speech level consistency within each track.** Check the register table in Section 6 before beginning any story. A CR story must be formal throughout; a PI story must be casual throughout.

3. **A0 vocabulary only.** This is a beginner course. Avoid complex grammar constructions, rare vocabulary, and idiomatic expressions that a beginner cannot understand from context. If you use a culturally adapted term that might be unfamiliar, it should be immediately clear from context.

4. **Use natural expressions.** The test: *Would a native speaker actually say this?* Textbook phrasing ("I would like to purchase a ticket, please") vs. natural phrasing ("Can I get a ticket?"). Always choose natural.

5. **Context-appropriate immersion.** A café scene should feel like a local café in Tokyo, New York, Mexico City, Paris, or Beijing — not a Korean café with translated labels. Adapt everything: what people order, how they order it, what the environment feels like.

6. **Preserve pedagogical goals.** The Goal field of each sim defines what the learner is practicing. Do not adapt in ways that change the communicative function. If the goal is "ask for directions," the adapted scene must also require asking for directions.

7. **Outro tone by track:** Maintain the emotional/tonal register of each track's Outro (see learnings.md for tone notes): PI = dramatic/fun; TR = practical/adventurous; KD = emotional/dramatic; KP = fan-culture excitement; CR = professional with dry humor.

8. **Do not invent content.** If a scene references a specific place, food, or cultural element, replace it with the mapped equivalent — do not introduce entirely new cultural content that doesn't appear in the Korean source.
